\section{Introduction} [1]
Movement variability is an inherent feature within and between persons. [11]
Research on measurement and understanding of movement variability has been [21]
well established in the last three decades in areas such as biomechanics, [33]
sport science, psychology, cognitive science, neuroscience and robotics [41]
\cite{2018arXiv181009249X}. [41]
Also, considering that methodologies for movement variability [48]
to quantify variability in facial expressions and [55]
my preliminary experiments of the analysis of variations [63]
for facial expressions using nonlinear dynamics \cite{MPXochicale_CERE2018}. [69]
I am interested in quantifying the complexity [76]
of facial expression that one person or multiple persons can present in scenarios [89]
of human-robot interaction and  in researching [95]
the subtle variations of facial expressions that are related to different [106]
mental states  (e.g. anxiety, disinterest, relief) \cite{back2014}. [112]
Hence, such statements have led me to ask two questions in the context [125]
of human-robot interaction: [128]
(i) does the quantification of the variation for facial expressions [138]
using nonlinear dynamics can tell us something about the state of mind of a person?, [153]
(ii) how the quantification of facial expressions can be related with [164]
the complexity of facial expressions?. [169]

\section{Methods} [1]
Considering the work in my Ph.D. thesis where investigated nonlinear dynamics [12]
to quantify movement variability \cite{XochicalePhDThesis2018}, [16]
I am proposing to apply Recurrence Quantification Analysis (RQA) for this work. [28]
RQA computes measurements based on the recurrence points density of diagonal [39]
or vertical line structures in Recurrence Plots. [46]
Such measurements of dynamics can determine the dynamics of a system, [57]
e.g. the determinism (predictability) or Shannon entropy (complexity) [65]
\cite{marwan2007}. [65]
Hence, the use Shannon Entropy can be applied to measure [75]
from the recurrent quantification analysis (also known as RQAEntr) [84]
to quantify the complexity of face expressions. [91]
